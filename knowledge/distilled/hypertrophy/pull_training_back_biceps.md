# Pull Training: Back & Biceps Optimization

## Key Principles
* **Pull Day Structure:** Focused on back (lats/rhomboids/traps), biceps, and rear delts.
* **Feeder Sets:** A warm-up protocol using 4-5 sets with gradually increasing RPE (4–10) to prepare for a heavy all-out set.
* **Lengthened-Position Hypertrophy:** Prioritizing training at the longest muscle length for superior growth.
* **Mechanical Tension & Mind-Muscle Connection:** Balancing heavy compound loading with isolation for optimal fiber recruitment.
* **Dropsets:** Used at the end of key exercises to drive activation past technical failure.
* **Asymmetry Mitigation:** Utilizing unilateral training to correct strength and size imbalances.
* **Bicep Bias:** Overhead curl variations preferentially target the long head (bicep peak).

## Coaching Notes
* **Feeder Sets vs. Warm-ups:** Feeder sets are essential for bottleneck movements like lat pulldowns where neurological priming is critical.
* **Grip Consistency:** Maintain identical grip width during all feeder sets to ensure accurate strength baseline tracking.
* **Fatigue Management:** Technical failure on back movements is often limited by secondary muscles; dropsets allow the primary back fibers to reach true mechanical fatigue.
* **Unilateral Focus:** If an exercise lacks "feel," switch to unilateral versions to remove momentum and re-establish mind-muscle connection.

## Beginner Mistakes
* **Grip Inconsistency:** Varying grip width during warm-ups, rendering strength tracking useless.
* **Premature Termination:** Stopping at technical failure instead of utilizing intensity techniques to push target fibers further.
* **Ignoring Asymmetries:** Neglecting unilateral work allows imbalances to compound over time.
* **Minimalist Warm-ups:** Skipping the necessary neurological priming for complex compound pulls.

## Advanced Insights
* **The 30% Rule:** After reaching technical failure on a major lift, strip 30% of the load and perform 4-5 additional controlled reps.
* **Bicep Peak Anatomy:** The long head of the bicep is best recruited when the shoulder is positioned such that the muscle is fully stretched (overhead).
