# Training Volume & Hypertrophy: A Knowledge Base

## Key Principles
* **Volume-Hypertrophy Relationship:** Weekly training volume follows an "Inverted-U" curve. More sets are beneficial only up to a threshold; excessive volume leads to diminishing returns and overtraining.
* **Training Age Scaling:** Recommendations must be categorized by experience:
    * **Beginner:** <1 year
    * **Intermediate:** 1–4 years
    * **Advanced:** >4 years
* **Effective vs. "Junk" Volume:** A set only counts toward your goal if performed with high intensity (close to failure). "Going through the motions" is ineffective junk volume.
* **Frequency:** Hitting each muscle group 2–3 times per week is generally superior for hypertrophy compared to once-a-week body-part splits.
* **Compound Scaling:** As training age increases, set counts must scale upward, while maintaining a balance between compound and isolation work.

## Scientific Takeaways
* **Frequency:** Increased training frequency (2-3x/week) outperforms lower frequency (1x/week) for hypertrophy when volume is equated. (Strong Evidence)
* **The Inverted-U Curve:** Weekly volume and hypertrophic gains follow an inverted-U trend. (Strong Evidence)
* **Newbie Gains:** Beginners are highly sensitive to novel stimuli, requiring significantly less volume than advanced trainees to stimulate growth. (Strong Evidence)

## Coaching Notes
* **Muscle-Centric Counting:** Track volume by muscles involved, not just exercises. (e.g., A Bench Press counts as a Chest, Front Delt, and Triceps set).
* **Adaptation-Based Programming:** A "Beginner" program is defined by the volume needed to stimulate adaptation, not complex exercises. Simple programs can yield advanced results with high effort.
* **Plateau Management:** Plateaus are best addressed by checking total weekly volume—often, reducing volume to a recoverable level and increasing per-set intensity is more effective than adding more work.

## Beginner Mistakes
* **Junk Volume:** Performing excessive sets (25+) without enough intensity to make them effective.
* **Inconsistent Counting:** Neglecting to account for compound lift carryover to secondary muscle groups.
* **Volume Blindness:** Treating intermediate/advanced training as "more of the same" rather than increasing overall intensity and management.
* **Ignoring Deloads:** Failing to plan reductions in volume when training is high.
* **Poor Split Selection:** Prioritizing "body part" splits over balanced Full Body or Push/Pull/Legs splits.

## Advanced Insights
* **Recommended Volume per Week:**
    * **Beginner:** 5–12 sets/muscle
    * **Intermediate:** 8–15 sets/muscle
    * **Advanced:** 12–25 sets/muscle
* **Intensity/Recovery:** Focus on high effort (RIR 1–3). If performance stalls, prioritize a deload rather than increasing set count.
* **Nutrition:** Support tissue repair with ~1.6–2.2g of protein per kg of body weight daily.

## App Implementation Ideas
* **Volume Tracker:** Automatically calculate weekly set totals per muscle group and flag "Junk Volume" territory (>20 sets/week).
* **Onboarding Logic:** Use an experience-level quiz to set default volume caps (Beginner: 5-12, Intermediate: 8-15, Advanced: 12-25) to prevent early overtraining.
* **Automated Set Attribution:** Use a "Set Counter" that automatically attributes a compound lift set to multiple muscle groups (e.g., Bench Press = Chest + Triceps + Front Delts) for accurate weekly totals.
