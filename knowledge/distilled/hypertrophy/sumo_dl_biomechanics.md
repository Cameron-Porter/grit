# Sumo Deadlift Biomechanics & Programming

## Key Principles
* **Sumo Biomechanics:** A wider stance and upright torso shift the mechanical demand away from the lower back (erector spinae) and place it heavily on the quadriceps and glutes.
* **The "Wedge" Technique:** Setup requires pulling the torso into a rigid, upright posture using the barbell as an anchor. Wedge the hips between the bar and the floor to create maximal full-body tension before the lift.
* **Push vs. Pull:** Conceptualize the lift as a "leg press" into the floor using the quadriceps, rather than a back pull.
* **Dead-Stop Execution:** Repetitions must come to a complete stop on the floor to reset tension. Touch-and-go (bouncing) reps compromise form and eliminate the hardest part of the lift.
* **The "Cheating" Debate:** While the absolute range of motion is shorter, the mechanical work is offset by the extreme difficulty of breaking the barbell off the floor from a disadvantaged starting position.

## Scientific Takeaways
* **Strong Evidence:** The sumo setup places a significantly higher demand on knee extension (quadriceps) compared to the conventional deadlift.
* **Strong Evidence:** The upright torso angle drastically reduces shear force and torque on the lumbar spine.
* **Moderate Evidence:** Despite a shorter vertical bar path, the overall mechanical work and hypertrophic effects are comparable to conventional deadlifting.
* **Expert Opinion:** Lighter weight-class athletes statistically favor sumo, while superheavyweights mechanically favor conventional pulls.

## Coaching Notes
* **Fixing Early Hip Rise:** Cue clients to "leg press the floor" and actively drive their knees out into their elbows during the initial pull.
* **Eccentric Knee Collisions:** If a client hits their knees on the way down, cue them to "break at the hips first" and delay knee bend until the bar passes the kneecap.
* **Stance Width:** Ideal width is highly individual based on hip anatomy. Start with shins on the rings and adjust inward/outward based on the ability to open hips pain-free.
* **Ban Touch-and-Go:** Force a full one-second pause on the ground between reps to re-brace the core and reset the lats.

## Beginner Mistakes
* Treating the lift like a wide-stance conventional deadlift (keeping hips too high and the torso horizontal).
* Letting the knees cave inward (valgus collapse) off the floor, leaking power and risking joint injury.
* Bouncing the plates off the floor instead of coming to a dead stop.
* Failing to "pull the slack out" before initiating the lift, causing a sudden jerk on the lower back.
* Looking straight at the ceiling or burying the chin. Maintain a neutral cervical spine by packing the neck ("double chin").

## Advanced Insights
* **Sets & Reps:** Program 3-5 sets of 3-8 reps. This is a highly technical, neurologically demanding lift; avoid high rep ranges to prevent fatigue-induced form breakdown.
* **Intensity:** Lift heavy (75-85%+ of 1RM) but maintain 1-2 Reps in Reserve (RIR). Taking technical compounds to absolute failure severely increases injury risk.
* **Frequency & Recovery:** Perform 1-2 times per week. Allow 48-72 hours of lower-body recovery. If lumbar fatigue is high, temporarily substitute with trap bar deadlifts.
* **Progression:** Prioritize strict micro-loading (+2.5 to 5 lbs/week) or adding 1 rep per set while keeping bar speed and form identical.
* **Exercise Selection:** Prioritize sumo if you have a history of lower back pain, short arms relative to your torso, or specifically want to target quad/glute hypertrophy while building pull strength.
