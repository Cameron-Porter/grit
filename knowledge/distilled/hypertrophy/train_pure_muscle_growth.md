# Training for Pure Muscle Growth (Hypertrophy)

## Key Principles
* **Tension is King:** Mechanical tension is the primary biochemical driver of muscle hypertrophy.
* **Bodybuilding Technique:** Prioritize controlled eccentrics and targeted tension over simply moving weight from point A to point B.
* **Stretch-Mediated Hypertrophy:** The stretched portion of a lift is the most hypertrophic aspect of the range of motion.
* **Effort & Failure:** Muscle growth spikes as sets approach momentary muscular failure.
* **Progressive Overload:** Continually force adaptation by increasing reps, weight, technique quality, or mind-muscle connection.
* **Sets & Reps:** Perform 2-3 sets per exercise. Use moderate-high reps (10-12) to add volume, lower reps (6) to add weight. 

## Scientific Takeaways
* **Strong Evidence:**
    * The eccentric (lowering) phase is more important for growth than the concentric phase.
    * Training volume is a primary driver of hypertrophy, up to a certain threshold.
    * Machines are at least as effective—sometimes superior—to free weights due to even resistance profiles.
* **Moderate Evidence:**
    * Slower eccentrics paired with fast, explosive concentrics maximize hypertrophy.
    * Partial reps performed exclusively in the stretched aspect of a lift are highly hypertrophic.
    * Training to absolute failure is not strictly superior to stopping just shy of it, largely due to total volume trade-offs.

## Coaching Notes
* **Manage Failure:** Taking every set to failure causes disproportionate fatigue and lowers total training volume. Leave 1-2 Reps in Reserve (RIR) on early sets; take only the final set to absolute failure.
* **Assess Plateaus:** Most trainees fail to see progress because they overestimate their intensity and do not train close enough to failure, not because of genetic limits.
* **Strict Execution:** Cheating shifts tension away from the target muscle to secondary movers or joints. Strict execution is non-negotiable.
* **Alternative Overload:** When load or reps cannot be added, cue clients to improve negative control or mind-muscle connection to continue overloading safely.

## Beginner Mistakes
* Using powerlifting technique (just moving weight) instead of prioritizing muscle tension.
* Letting the weight free-fall during the eccentric phase, missing the most hypertrophic part of the rep.
* Neglecting the fully stretched position of the target muscle at the bottom of the movement.
* Taking heavy compound lifts (squats, deadlifts) to absolute failure, frying the central nervous system.
* Rushing rest periods (under 1 minute) to chase a pump rather than allowing recovery for mechanical tension.

## Advanced Insights
* **Stimulus-to-Fatigue Ratio (SFR):** Exercise selection must maximize target muscle tension (stimulus) while minimizing systemic and joint strain (fatigue). Heavy barbell compounds have a high fatigue cost; machine alternatives (e.g., hack squats) offer better SFR for pure hypertrophy.
* **Resistance Profiles:** Match the exercise's difficulty curve with a muscle's natural strength curve. Cables offer continuous tension, whereas free weights often have "dead zones" (e.g., the bottom of a dumbbell curl).

## App Implementation Ideas
* **Workout Pathways:** Dynamically filter exercise libraries for 'Hypertrophy' tracks to prioritize high-SFR movements (cables/machines) over heavy free weights.
* **Dynamic RIR Programming:** Prompt users during multi-set exercises (e.g., "Sets 1-2: Leave 2 reps in the tank. Set 3: AMRAP/Failure.").
* **Pacing & Tempo UI:** Embed visual metronomes or haptic feedback in the workout player (e.g., a 3-second visual countdown for the eccentric phase) alongside pop-up cues emphasizing the "deep stretch."
* **Expanded Progression Tracking:** Allow users to log improvements in non-weight metrics like 'Form Quality', 'Negative Control', or 'Mind-Muscle Connection'.
