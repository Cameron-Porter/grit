# Progressive Overload & Training Progression

## Key Principles
* **Progressive Overload:** The fundamental driver of hypertrophy; consistently increasing the training stimulus over time.
* **Micro-loading:** Utilizing minimal weight increments (e.g., 2.5–5 lbs) to ensure continuous, sustainable progression.
* **Rep-Range Progression:** Prioritizing increasing reps within a target range before increasing weight load.
* **RPE/RIR Management:** Adjusting intensity to match fatigue levels while maintaining a productive stimulus (1–3 RIR).
* **Periodization (Mesocycle):** Structuring blocks to include progressive overload followed by a planned deload to allow supercompensation.
* **Technical Quality:** The non-negotiable ceiling for progression; if form breaks down, the stimulus is no longer effective or safe.

## Scientific Takeaways
| Claim | Evidence Level | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Overload Mechanism | Strong | Hypertrophy is primarily a result of increasing mechanical tension over time. |
| Volume/Load Relation | Strong | Increasing total volume (sets x reps x load) reliably correlates with hypertrophy. |
| Supercompensation | Moderate | Planned periods of reduced volume/intensity are required to realize long-term growth. |

## Coaching Notes
* **Consistency over Perfection:** A mediocre program followed consistently beats a perfect program followed sporadically.
* **Inconsistent Effort:** The most common cause of stalled progress is "coasting"—failing to treat today's weights with the same intensity as last week's.
* **Rep Progression:** If adding weight is not possible, adding a single high-quality rep to a set constitutes valid progress.
* **Basics First:** Avoid chasing "new" or "novel" exercises to solve plateaus; instead, master the basics and drive up the load on them.

## Beginner Mistakes
* **Randomization:** Changing exercises every session makes tracking load progression impossible.
* **Weight over Form:** Increasing weight too quickly, forcing form breakdown to hit target rep counts.
* **Deload Neglect:** Ignoring planned deloads, leading to systemic fatigue and stagnation.
* **Tracking Failure:** Failing to log intensity (RIR), resulting in "junk volume" sets that lack sufficient mechanical tension.
* **Comparative Bias:** Focusing on others' progress rather than personal incremental gains.

## Advanced Insights
* **Autoregulation:** Intensity is a tool, not a constant. Adjust loads based on current fatigue levels to maintain the productive 1–3 RIR range.
* **Minimum Viable Progression:** Progress doesn't need to be massive. Small, measurable gains per session are the most reliable path to long-term adaptation.

## App Implementation Ideas
* **Progression Engine:** Auto-check performance against set rep targets (e.g., 6–10 reps). If hit, prompt for load increase.
* **Autoregulation Toggle:** Allow RIR logging; if fatigue is high, the app suppresses load-increase suggestions to prevent injury.
* **Deload Automation:** Trigger automated deload prompts after 6 weeks of continuous logged data.
