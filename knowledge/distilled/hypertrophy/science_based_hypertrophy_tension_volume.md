# Science-Based Hypertrophy: Principles of Tension and Volume

## Key Principles
* **Mechanical Tension:** The primary driver of hypertrophy. Maximizing tension requires handling challenging loads through a full range of motion.
* **Progressive Overload:** The necessity of consistently increasing mechanical demand (weight, reps, or sets) over time to force adaptation.
* **Volume Thresholds:** 10–20 effective sets per muscle group per week provides the highest return on investment for most lifters.
* **Proximity to Failure:** Sets must be taken within 1–3 Reps in Reserve (RIR) to ensure recruitment of high-threshold motor units.
* **Nutritional Foundation:** Protein (1.6–2.2g/kg body weight) is the essential building block; without it, training stimuli cannot be fully realized.

## Scientific Takeaways
* **Tension as Trigger:** Mechanical tension is the primary trigger for muscle protein synthesis (mTOR activation). (Strong Evidence)
* **Inverted-U Volume Curve:** Volume follows an inverted-U curve; beyond 20 sets per muscle/week, recovery capacity is often outpaced by fatigue. (Strong Evidence)
* **Training Frequency:** Training muscles 2+ times per week is superior to 1x per week for long-term hypertrophy. (Strong Evidence)

## Coaching Notes
* **The Logbook Rule:** If you aren't tracking load and reps, you are exercising, not training. Growth requires objective data.
* **Quality vs. Quantity:** One set taken to 1 RIR is worth more than three sets taken to 5+ RIR.
* **Movement Quality:** Range of motion is a component of tension. A half-rep with 200lbs provides less tension to the target tissue than a full-rep with 180lbs.

## Beginner Mistakes
* **Junk Volume:** Performing many sets with very low intensity, mistakenly believing "more time in the gym" equals "more muscle."
* **Program Hopping:** Switching exercises weekly, preventing the body from mastering movements and achieving true progressive overload.
* **Ignoring Protein:** Relying on training stimuli while failing to provide the amino acids required for tissue repair.
* **Ego Lifting:** Using momentum and partial range of motion to handle weights the target muscle cannot actually control.

## Advanced Insights
* **Programming:** Perform 10–20 working sets per muscle group weekly. While rep ranges can vary (5–30), 6–12 is efficient for balancing load and metabolic stress.
* **Intensity:** Train close to failure (1–3 RIR). If a set doesn't feel challenging enough to prevent an additional 1–3 clean reps, the intensity is likely too low.
* **Double Progression:** Stick to a weight until the top of the rep range is reached for all sets, then increase the load by the smallest increment possible.
* **Recovery:** Cap volume at 20 sets per muscle weekly. If strength stalls or persistent soreness occurs, initiate a deload week (reducing volume by 50%).

## App Implementation Ideas
* **Weekly Volume Dashboard:** Automatically calculate sets per muscle group and flag when a user exceeds 20 sets to prevent systemic overtraining.
* **Tension Check:** Trigger a prompt after every logged set ("Did you use full ROM?" and "Was this challenging?") to encourage quality control.
* **Nutrition Sync:** Track protein intake against the 0.7g-1.0g/lb target, pushing alerts if daily intake drops below the maintenance floor.
