# G.R.I.T. Doctrine: Exercise Selection

> Canonical reference for exercise selection principles, SFR evaluation, and movement prioritization in the G.R.I.T. platform.

## Core Principles

### 1. Stimulus-to-Fatigue Ratio (SFR) is the Ultimate Selection Metric
An exercise is valuable in direct proportion to the hypertrophic stimulus it produces relative to the fatigue it generates. An exercise that produces intense localized stimulus with minimal systemic fatigue is superior to one that produces moderate stimulus with high fatigue, even if the second exercise is considered "more effective" by general reputation. The deadlift, for example, is an extraordinary lower back and hip hinge builder, but its systemic fatigue cost is so high that its SFR for hypertrophy (as opposed to strength) is lower than many alternatives.

### 2. Load the Stretch Position
The most potent hypertrophic signal comes from mechanical tension at the muscle's longest functional length. Exercises that maximize the stretched position under load outperform those that emphasize peak contraction or mid-range only. This is the primary reason stretch-emphasis variations (deficit push-ups, Romanian deadlifts, dumbbell flyes with full range, cable lateral raises) outperform comparable exercises that truncate the stretched range.

### 3. The Target Muscle Must be the Limiting Factor
An exercise only trains what fails first. If the hands, wrists, or lower back fatigue before the target muscle, the exercise is not stimulating the intended muscle to its capacity. Exercise selection must ensure the target muscle is the limiter — the factor that determines when the set ends. Any technique modification, equipment choice, or exercise swap that shifts fatigue to the target muscle improves selection quality.

### 4. SFR is Individual
Published "best exercise" lists are starting points, not prescriptions. An exercise that produces excellent stimulus signals for one lifter (localized burn, pump, and DOMS in the target muscle) may produce only joint discomfort and grip fatigue for another. Every exercise recommendation must be tested on the individual before being encoded as their "best" option. The test period is 3–4 weeks of honest effort.

### 5. Exercise Rotation Prevents Accommodation
Adaptation to a specific movement pattern occurs within 4–8 weeks. This is not muscle adaptation (muscle continues to grow if overload continues) but neural and connective tissue accommodation to the specific loading pattern. Rotating exercises at mesocycle boundaries introduces new stimulus while preserving the progressive overload logic. Changing exercises mid-mesocycle without reason disrupts overload tracking.

## Rules

### Primary Selection Criteria (in order of priority)
1. **Target muscle is the limiting factor** — if not, swap the exercise
2. **Stretch position is loaded under tension** — exercises that cut the ROM short sacrifice the most productive portion of the lift
3. **Full ROM is achievable with current mobility** — a full-range exercise performed poorly is inferior to a reduced-range exercise performed well, but the goal is full range with good technique
4. **SFR is sustainable** — the exercise must be repeatable across multiple sessions without cumulative joint degradation
5. **Equipment is available** — the best exercise on a list that cannot be performed is irrelevant

### Stimulus Signals (Positive SFR Indicators)
When an exercise is working correctly, the following signals should be present:
- **Localized pump**: blood and metabolic accumulation specifically in the target muscle, not surrounding areas
- **Localized burn**: burning/fatigue sensation isolated to the target muscle, not joints or tendons
- **Acute weakness**: the target muscle fails first, leaving the lifter unable to continue despite energy and cardiovascular capacity remaining
- **Appropriate DOMS**: delayed onset muscle soreness located in the target muscle 24–48 hours post-session (note: absence of DOMS in experienced lifters does not indicate poor selection — DOMS reflects novelty, not stimulus)

### Fatigue Signals (Negative SFR Indicators)
These indicate the exercise may need to be replaced or modified:
- Joint pain or tendon irritation during or after the movement
- Grip, wrist, or stabilizer fatigue limits the set before the target muscle is challenged
- Psychological dread or avoidance — sustainable SFR includes mental sustainability
- No discernible pump or burn in the target muscle after multiple attempts across weeks

### Exercise Tier System (by Role)
| Role | Description | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Main compound for the muscle group | Multi-joint, high mechanical load, stretch position accessible, 5–12 rep default |
| Secondary | Supporting compound or compound variation | May be unilateral, different angle, moderate mechanical load, 8–15 rep default |
| Accessory | Isolation or stretch-emphasis exercise | Single-joint, lower systemic fatigue, high localized stimulus, 10–20 rep default |

**Primary slots**: Always filled first. The session's anchor. Compounds or stretch-emphasis compounds.

**Secondary slots**: Added for emphasized/grow-priority muscles. Can be variations of the primary pattern (incline vs. flat), unilateral versions, or different angles.

**Accessory slots**: Added for MEV-level muscles or as supplemental volume. Isolations, cable variations, high-SFR movements.

### Movement Priority by Muscle Group

**Chest**
- Primary: Horizontal press with full pec stretch (flat or slight decline; dumbbell preferred for ROM)
- Secondary: Incline press (upper pec emphasis)
- Accessory: Cable fly or pec deck with full stretch at the open position

**Back**
- Primary: Vertical pull (lat pulldown / pull-up) — full lat stretch at top
- Secondary: Horizontal row (chest-supported preferred to remove lower back from the equation)
- Accessory: Straight-arm pulldown, cable pullover (isolates lat without bicep involvement)

**Shoulders (Delts)**
- Primary: Overhead press — compound, full overhead ROM (primarily develops anterior deltoid; does not replace lateral delt work)
- Secondary: Cable or machine lateral raise — forward lean, 15–30° elbow bend, 2-second eccentric, 10–20 reps; **cables are preferred over dumbbells** because dumbbells provide zero tension at the bottom (the most hypertrophically valuable stretched position), while cables maintain continuous tension throughout
- Accessory: Rear delt fly — face pull, cable rear delt fly, or machine reverse fly (transverse plane required for posterior delt; overhead press does not train it)
- **Technique critical for lateral raises**: Keep wrist neutral (do not "pour the pitcher" / internally rotate the shoulder); raise in the scapular plane (15–30° forward of the frontal plane); stop at shoulder height — raising above shifts load to upper traps and narrows the subacromial space, increasing impingement risk. Internal rotation during lateral raises is rated Strong Evidence as a subacromial impingement mechanism
- **Multi-angle coverage required**: The deltoid has 7 functional anatomical segments (not 3 heads); complete delt development requires abduction-plane isolation (lateral delt), overhead pressing (anterior delt), and transverse-plane movements (rear delt) — no single exercise covers all segments
- **Avoid**: heavy barbell lateral raises, upright rows with narrow grip (shoulder impingement risk)

**Biceps**
- Primary: Supinated curl with full elbow extension at bottom (stretch position)
- Secondary: Incline dumbbell curl or preacher curl (emphasizes stretch or peak contraction differently)
- **Best SFR**: Exercises that allow full elbow extension under load (incline curl, spider curl)

**Triceps**
- Primary: Overhead extension (tricep in fully stretched position) — skull crusher, cable overhead extension
- Secondary: Pushdown (mid-range to contraction) — tricep rope pushdown, cable bar pushdown
- **Avoid**: Close-grip bench as primary tricep builder — its SFR for triceps is low relative to systemic fatigue cost

**Quads**
- Primary: Deep squat pattern — barbell back squat (high bar), front squat, heel-elevated squat, leg press with deep ROM
- Secondary: Unilateral — Bulgarian split squat, goblet squat (increases per-quad stimulus, reduces systemic load)
- Accessory: Leg extension (pure isolation at knee joint)
- **Critical**: ROM is the primary driver of quad development — depth matters more than load
- Upright torso (heel elevation or high-bar position) maximizes anterior thigh tension

**Hamstrings**
- Primary: Hip hinge with full hip extension — Romanian deadlift, stiff-leg deadlift (hamstring stretch at bottom is the productive range)
- Secondary: Leg curl — **seated leg curl is preferred over lying leg curl** because the seated position places the hip in flexion, training the hamstring at a longer overall muscle length (hip flexion + knee extension stretch simultaneously). This superiority is rated Strong Evidence. Lying leg curls are acceptable alternatives but sub-optimal when seated is available
- **Best SFR**: Exercises that provide tension at the hip with knee extended (stretched position)

**Glutes**
- Primary: Hip thrust or barbell glute bridge — maximum hip extension under load
- Secondary: Deficit reverse lunge, hip extension with cable — constant tension options
- Accessory: Cable kickback, hip abduction machine

**Calves**
- Primary: Standing calf raise — gastrocnemius (stretched with straight knee)
- Secondary: Seated calf raise — soleus (isolated with bent knee)
- **Critical**: Full range of motion at the bottom (complete stretch) is necessary; partial range calves training is nearly universally performed and nearly universally suboptimal

**Abs**
- Primary: Weighted spinal flexion — cable crunch, hanging leg raise, decline weighted crunch
- Secondary: Core stabilization — dead bug, Pallof press (resist extension/rotation rather than flex)
- **Rule**: Abs require mechanical tension and progressive overload just like any other muscle — bodyweight crunches with no progression do not build abs

## Practical Application

### Exercise Pool per Muscle Group (for G.R.I.T. Slot System)
The approved exercise list per slot should include 3–6 options that represent:
- Different equipment availability (barbell, dumbbell, cable, machine, bodyweight)
- Different angles or positions for the same muscle function
- At minimum one stretch-emphasis option per muscle

This allows the Exercise Picker to provide options regardless of equipment and allows individual SFR testing across variants.

### Individual SFR Testing Protocol
When introducing a new exercise:
1. Perform 3 sets in the primary slot for **3–4 consecutive sessions**
2. After each session, note: localized pump present? Localized burn present? Target muscle failed first?
3. Check DOMS location 24–48 hours post-session: in the target muscle, adjacent muscles, or nowhere?
4. If stimulus signals are absent after 3–4 sessions with correct technique: retire the exercise and test an alternative
5. If stimulus signals are strong: encode as a primary or secondary option for this individual

### Selecting for Equipment Constraints
**Barbell available**: Prioritize barbell compounds for Primary slots (highest mechanical load capacity)
**Dumbbells only**: Substitute dumbbell versions of all compounds — equivalent hypertrophy at matched volume; advantage of greater ROM in most movements
**Cables**: Preferred for accessory/isolation slots — constant tension through the full range of motion, superior to free weights for lateral raises, flyes, and pulldowns. For lateral raises specifically, cables are the highest-SFR option because they provide tension at the bottom of the movement (the stretched position) where dumbbells provide none (Strong Evidence)
**Machines**: Appropriate for all slots, particularly when joint stability is a limitation; machines allow higher effort without stabilizer fatigue limiting the set

### When to Rotate Exercises
- **Mandatory**: at every mesocycle boundary (4–8 weeks)
- **Triggered**: when an exercise stops producing stimulus signals for 2+ consecutive weeks (accommodation)
- **Triggered**: when joint pain or tendon irritation is associated specifically with an exercise
- **Avoid**: rotating exercises mid-mesocycle for novelty without cause — this resets progression data

## Disagreements in the Source Material

**Pre-exhaust isolation before compound (Schwarzenegger/Haney) vs. compound-first (Nippard/Israetel for beginners)**
- Pre-exhaust increases isolation of the target muscle during the compound by pre-fatiguing it, reducing joint/stabilizer involvement
- Compound-first maximizes neural drive and load capacity for the primary movement
- **G.R.I.T. position**: Compound-first for beginners and intermediates (Primary slot first). Pre-exhaust is an advanced technique for experienced lifters managing specific joints — not a general default.

**Barbell vs. dumbbell vs. machine (superiority claims)**
- Multiple sources cite equivalent hypertrophy at matched volume across equipment types
- Free weights build stabilizer strength; machines allow higher target muscle stimulus with less stabilizer limitation
- **G.R.I.T. position**: No equipment is universally superior. Select based on individual SFR, availability, and the specific movement's ROM requirements.

**Rep range for delts: 5–8 heavy vs. 10–25**
- Heavy overhead pressing for delts (5–8 reps) is advocated by some sources for compound shoulder development
- Higher-rep lateral raises (10–25) are consistently recommended for side delt isolation
- Optimal Delt Growth file explicitly flags 5–8 rep lateral raises as causing form breakdown and joint irritation
- **G.R.I.T. position**: Heavy overhead pressing acceptable where shoulder health permits; isolation movements for delts default to 10–20 reps. Do not prescribe heavy lateral raises.

## Outdated Guidance

**Fixed "best exercise" lists that ignore individual variation**: No single exercise is objectively best for everyone. The SFR for any given movement is individual. Published rankings of "top 3 chest exercises" are starting points for testing, not prescriptions.

**Upright rows as primary delt builder**: High-risk for supraspinatus impingement, especially with narrow grip. The movement pattern places the shoulder in a position associated with impingement syndrome. Lateral raises and overhead pressing are superior SFR options without the injury risk.

**Behind-the-neck pressing**: Places the shoulder in external rotation under heavy load — a known risk factor for shoulder impingement. No meaningful performance advantage over front-of-neck pressing for hypertrophy.

**Smith machine squats as quad isolation**: Artificially altering the bar path changes the load distribution across joints and reduces quad stimulus relative to natural barbell squats or leg press. Machine squats are not a substitute for free-form squats in quad development.

**Partial range of motion as intentional strategy (not injury accommodation)**: Shortened-position partials (stopping before the muscle reaches its full stretch) as a deliberate hypertrophy strategy produce inferior muscle development compared to full ROM training. The stretch position is the most productive range — cutting it short abandons the highest-value portion of the rep. *Qualification added 2026-06-12:* This prohibition applies to shortened-position partials only. Lengthened-position partials — performed in the stretched portion of the ROM, typically added after reaching full-ROM failure — are validated by Strong Evidence (2026 IJES study; multiple convergent files) as producing near-equivalent hypertrophy to full ROM at matched volume. These are an intensity technique, not a ROM shortcut. The rule remains: never truncate the stretch; but adding stretched-position partials after full-ROM failure is a legitimate extension of the set, not a violation of this principle.
