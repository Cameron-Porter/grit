# G.R.I.T. Doctrine: Progression

> Canonical reference for progression models, advancement methods, and overload strategies in the G.R.I.T. platform.
> Sources: Israetel (RP), Nippard, Schwarzenegger/Haney. Primarily derived from training_for_muscle_growth_beginner_to_advanced.md, optimal_rep_range_for_hypertrophy.md, dumbbell_hypertrophy.md, dumbbell_leg_hypertrophy.md, time_limited_training.md, volume_landmarks.md, deloading_protocols.md.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Progressive Overload is the Only Non-Negotiable
Every other training variable — volume, frequency, exercise selection, technique — exists in service of progressive overload. Without a trackable increase in training stimulus over time, no adaptation occurs. A program that does not produce progression in some measurable variable within a mesocycle is not working, regardless of how well-designed it appears.

### 2. Track Everything; Guess Nothing
Progression is only visible when baseline data exists. Every working set must be logged: load, reps, RIR. Week-over-week comparison is the only objective measure of whether the program is producing adaptation. Training from memory produces inconsistency that is interpreted as progress or plateau inaccurately.

### 3. Reps Before Load
The default progression model for hypertrophy: add reps within a target range before adding load. This ensures the muscle is being maximally stimulated at the current load before demanding more. Adding load prematurely compresses technique and causes disproportionate fatigue before the muscle has fully adapted.

### 4. Progression Speed is Inversely Related to Training Age
Beginners add weight session to session. Intermediates add weight week to week. Advanced lifters add weight month to month (or cycle to cycle). Expecting beginner-rate progression as an advanced lifter produces frustration and program hopping. Expecting advanced-rate progression as a beginner is leaving significant gains on the table.

### 5. Every Progression Method Has a Ceiling
Linear progression (add weight every session) eventually fails for everyone. Double progression (max reps before adding weight) eventually fails. When a progression method stops working, the solution is a new mesocycle or method — not more of the same approach.

## Rules

### Progression by Experience Level

**Beginner (0–2 years training)**
- Primary method: **Linear progression** — add weight when the top of the rep range is achieved with clean technique
- Rate: 5–10 lbs per session for lower body compounds; 2.5–5 lbs per session for upper body compounds; 1.25–2.5 lbs for isolations
- Rep range: 5–10 (narrow range — technique is still being grooved; high reps with form breakdown produce no stimulus and bad habits). *Qualification added 2026-06-12 (C-4):* Multiple sources (science_rep_ranges.md, foundational_strength_training.md) recommend 8–12 as the beginner starting range, citing better technique acquisition and reduced discomfort compared to 5–8. The 5–10 range is defensible but errs aggressive on the lower bound. When in doubt, default beginner isolation work to 8–12 rather than forcing 5 reps; consider 6–12 as a reasonable middle ground
- RIR tracking: do not use — track by bar speed; when bar slows noticeably, the set is at or near failure
- Do not change exercises for at least **3–4 months** — technique adaptation requires repetition with the same movements
- Focus: 2–4 distributed compound movements per session, full-body or upper/lower split

**Intermediate (3–6 years training)**
- Primary method: **Double progression** — progress reps to the top of the target range, then increase load and reset to the bottom of the range
- Example: target range 8–12 reps → hit 12 clean reps at 100 lbs → next session use 102.5 lbs, targeting 8+ reps
- Rate: load increases every **1–3 weeks**
- Rep range: expand to 5–15 across different exercises; 15–20 acceptable for isolation work
- RIR tracking: begin using 1–3 RIR targets; estimate within ~2 reps accuracy expected
- Introduce exercise rotation at mesocycle boundaries (every 4–8 weeks) to prevent accommodation
- 3–5 working sets per exercise appropriate; begin distinguishing warm-up sets from working sets

**Advanced (7+ years training)**
- Primary method: **Periodized double progression** — structured within a mesocycle (progressive volume overload week-to-week, deload, reset at higher baseline)
- Rate: load increases measured in **4–8 week blocks**, not sessions
- Micro-loading essential for isolation movements: **1.25 lb increments** when standard plate jumps (2.5 lbs) would exceed productive adaptation rate
- RIR tracking: precise to within ~1 rep; use RIR to drive intra-session volume decisions
- SFR-based exercise selection: retire movements that no longer produce clear stimulus signals (pump, localized fatigue, targeted DOMS) in favor of alternatives
- MRV awareness: track whether adding more volume continues to produce adaptation or begins causing performance regression

### Progression Methods (Toolkit)

| Method | When to Use | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Linear load increase | Beginners; early intermediates | Muscle is naive; any additional load produces adaptation |
| Double progression (reps → load) | Intermediates | Maximizes stimulus at current load before demanding more |
| Rep reset with load increase | After hitting top of rep band | Restores stimulus by changing the position within the rep range |
| Eccentric tempo manipulation | When load is capped (dumbbells, home gym) | Increases time under tension without requiring heavier weight |
| Pause at stretch position | When load is capped or movement is too easy | Amplifies stretch-mediated stimulus |
| Rest period reduction | Time-constrained training | Increases relative intensity without load change |
| Myo-rep matching | Advanced, time-constrained, isolation movements | Matches effective reps while reducing total set time |
| Lengthened-position partials | After reaching full-ROM failure; isolation exercises; intermediate/advanced | Extends effective stimulus beyond full-ROM failure by working the most productive (stretched) range of the rep |
| Volume overload (add sets) | Within a mesocycle | Increases total weekly stimulus; must be within MRV |

### Rep Range Progression Model
```
Week 1: 3 sets × 8 reps @ 100 lbs [RIR 2–3]
Week 2: 3 sets × 9-10 reps @ 100 lbs [RIR 2]
Week 3: 3 sets × 11-12 reps @ 100 lbs [RIR 1]
Week 4: Deload
Week 5 (new mesocycle): 3 sets × 8 reps @ 102.5 lbs [RIR 2–3]
```
This is the canonical double progression cycle.

### Volume Progression Within a Mesocycle
- Week 1: start at MEV or slightly above (~10 sets/muscle/week)
- Each subsequent week: add **1–2 sets per muscle per week**
- When recovery markers begin declining (joint ache, sleep quality, performance plateau), the lifter has approached MRV — do not add further volume
- Deload week: drop to ~50% of peak mesocycle volume
- Next mesocycle: begin at the previous mesocycle's Week 1 volume or slightly above

### Progression Blockers (When Progression Stalls)
| Symptom | Likely Cause | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Same reps/load for 3+ sessions | Accumulated fatigue masking fitness | Evaluate deload timing |
| Performance regression across sessions | Exceeded MRV or systemic overload | Immediate deload; reduce total volume next mesocycle |
| Reps increasing but load never increases | Stuck at bottom of rep range | Increase load; accept short-term rep drop |
| Progress on some muscles, not others | Under-prioritizing lagging muscles | Increase frequency and volume for lagging muscles next mesocycle |
| Progress in gym but muscle not growing | RIR too conservative; sets not hard enough | Tighten RIR; ensure genuine proximity to failure |

## Practical Application

### Session Logging Minimum Requirements
Every working set must record:
- Exercise name
- Load (weight used)
- Reps completed
- RIR (or subjective effort if beginner)
- Optional: notes on technique, fatigue level, notable observations

Without this data, progression tracking is impossible.

### Progression in Constrained Equipment Scenarios
**Dumbbells only / fixed-weight equipment**:
- Exhaust rep-based progression first (hit top of rep range with perfect form)
- Extend eccentric to 3–4 seconds when reps are capped
- Add a 0.5–1 second pause at the stretch position
- Reduce rest time between sets to increase relative intensity
- Perform more total sets (within session cap) rather than increasing load

**Bodyweight-only training**:
- Progress through movement difficulty scaling (e.g., push-up → archer push-up → deficit push-up → ring push-up)
- Rep-based progression with consistent tempo
- Load vest or resistance band attachment where possible

### When to Change Exercises
- At every mesocycle boundary (every 4–8 weeks) for intermediate and advanced lifters
- When an exercise stops producing the expected stimulus signals (pump, burn, DOMS in the target muscle) for 2+ consecutive weeks
- When an exercise causes joint pain, tendon irritation, or technique breakdown despite adequate load management
- Do not change exercises mid-mesocycle unless injury forces it — accommodation data needs time to accumulate

### Asymmetrical A/B Split Design (2× Weekly Frequency)
*Added 2026-06-12 (A-9):* For lifters training a muscle group 2× per week, both sessions should not be identical. Differentiating the two sessions by angle, load emphasis, or exercise selection improves regional hypertrophy and reduces overuse compared to running identical sessions twice. Examples: Chest-focused Push Day A + Delt-focused Push Day B; Lat-focused Pull Day A + Mid-back/rear-delt Pull Day B. (Strong Evidence for angle/region targeting; asymmetrical_ppl_splits.md, pull_day_architecture.md, building_delts_hypertrophy.md) This is a design consideration for intermediate and advanced lifters — not required for beginners who should train with identical sessions for technique consistency.

## Exceptions

**Plateau in fat loss phase**: Progression expectations must be reduced during a caloric deficit. Maintaining current load and reps (rather than adding) is a success during a cut. Treat muscle retention as the primary goal; any load increase is a bonus.

**Post-deload performance expression**: Expect a brief period of higher-than-normal performance in the first 1–2 sessions post-deload as fatigue dissipates and fitness is expressed. Do not mistake this for sustained progression — it is the deload working correctly.

**Deload week**: There is no progression during a deload week. Deload weeks exist to facilitate future progression, not current. Do not chase PRs during deloads.

**Extreme beginner (first 4–8 weeks)**: Absolute beginners progress neurally, not structurally, for the first several weeks. Early strength gains reflect neural efficiency improvements, not muscle growth. This phase requires technique focus above all; load management is secondary.

## Outdated Guidance

**"Muscle confusion"**: Constantly changing exercises does not stimulate "new" growth — it prevents accommodation data from accumulating and disrupts progressive overload tracking. Exercise variation has value at mesocycle boundaries, not within mesocycles.

**"More is always better"**: Volume has a ceiling. Beyond MRV, adding more sets produces regression, not adaptation. High-volume training has value only when it remains within recoverable range.

**"If you're not failing, you're not growing"**: Training to absolute muscular failure every set is not optimal. It generates disproportionate fatigue relative to stimulus. 1–2 RIR produces equivalent or superior stimulus in most conditions. Occasional failure sets are acceptable; every-set failure is a fatigue miscalculation. *Exception noted 2026-06-12:* HIT/Min-Max protocols (1–2 sets to absolute failure) are a valid alternative for advanced lifters, producing equivalent hypertrophy with fewer total sets. This is not contradictory — it is a different trade-off: maximum intensity on minimum sets versus moderate intensity on multiple sets. See foundational_beliefs.md R-01 qualification.

**"You must always add weight"**: During phases of high life stress, poor sleep, illness, or fat loss, maintaining performance is a valid and successful outcome. Forcing load increases when recovery is compromised produces injury, not adaptation.
